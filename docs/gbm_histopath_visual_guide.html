🔬 How do you spot a tumor in a histopathology slide?

A visual guide to what pathologists (and AI) look for in H&E stained GBM tissue — colour, shape, density, and structure

Step 0 — What is H&E staining?

Before you can read a slide, you need to understand the two dyes used. Every tissue slide in this challenge is stained with Haematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) — the most common stain in pathology. Each dye targets a different part of the cell.

Haematoxylin — the purple/blue dye
Stains: cell nuclei (DNA)
Anything with DNA turns dark purple/blue. In a normal brain there are relatively few nuclei per area. In a tumor, nuclei are packed densely — so a crowded purple patch = suspicious. Nuclei also change shape: normal ones are round and regular; tumor nuclei are irregular, enlarged, and sometimes bizarre-shaped.
Eosin — the pink/red dye
Stains: cytoplasm, proteins, connective tissue
Everything else turns pink — cell bodies, blood, collagen fibers, brain tissue. In necrotic (dead) zones, the pink eosin signal becomes pale and ghost-like because the protein structure has broken down. Blood vessels stain bright pink-red. Normal brain tissue is a pale pink background.

The golden rule: In a normal brain, you see few purple nuclei scattered in pale pink. In a GBM tumor, purple nuclei become dense, irregular, and clustered — because cancer cells are dividing rapidly and crowding into spaces where they don't belong. When you see a sudden shift from sparse pale pink to dense dark purple → that boundary is where you look first.

Normal brain vs GBM tumor — the core visual difference
✅ Normal brain tissue
Sparse nuclei, uniform pink background
🔴 GBM cellular tumor (CT)
Dense irregular nuclei, crowded, no empty space
Visual cues for each class — what to look for
0
CT
Cellular Tumor (CT)
The main tumor mass — most common class (43,401 patches)
🟣
Colour
Overwhelmingly dark purple/blue. Densely stained nuclei crowd out the pink background. Almost no white space.
📐
Shape / arrangement
Nuclei are irregular, enlarged, pleomorphic (variable shapes). Some nuclei look like they're splitting (mitotic figures). Cells packed with almost no gap.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Uniform dense purple across the whole patch. No clear dead zones, no ring structures. Just wall-to-wall cells. Looks "congested".
5
NC
Geographic Necrosis (NC)
Dead tissue zones — large, pale, ghost-like areas
⬜
Colour
Pale pink to almost white/grey. Dead cells lose their purple staining because DNA breaks down. Eosin (pink) also fades. Ghostly, washed-out appearance.
💀
Shape / arrangement
Ghost outlines of cells — you can see the shape of where cells were but no nucleus inside. "Coagulative necrosis" — the scaffold remains but the contents are gone. Irregular large zones.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Large pale irregular lakes surrounded by darker tissue. Looks like a bleached-out patch in the middle of purple. No active cells inside — just debris.
7
PN
Pseudopalisading Necrosis (PN)
The classic GBM hallmark — cells forming a "fence" around dead zones
🟤
Colour
Dark purple ring surrounding a pale/white centre. The dead zone is pale; the palisading cells around it are intensely purple.
🔵
Shape / arrangement
Cells line up in parallel rows like a picket fence around a central dead area. "Garland-like" or "wreath-like" arrangement. Nuclei orient perpendicular to the necrotic edge.
🗺️
Overall pattern
A pale central zone with concentric rings of dense purple cells around it. The bull's-eye appearance is nearly pathognomonic (exclusively diagnostic) for GBM. This is the most dramatic-looking sub-region.
4
MP
Microvascular Proliferation (MP)
Abnormal new blood vessels — "glomeruloid" structures
🔴
Colour
Bright pink-red channels (blood vessels) on a purple/pink background. The vessel walls stain intensely pink. Red blood cells inside stain bright red.
🌀
Shape / arrangement
Coiled, tangled, glomeruloid structures — looks like a little kidney glomerulus (a ball of knotted vessels). Multiple layers of endothelial cells stacking up. Abnormal, not the clean single-layer wall of a normal vessel.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Bright pink tortuous vessel clusters dotted through the tissue. Each cluster is small (~50–200µm) but very obvious — stands out as a hot pink knot in the purple/pink background. Multiple clusters per patch.
1
DM
Dense Macrophages (DM)
Immune cells eating debris — kidney-bean shaped nuclei
🫘
Colour
Medium purple nuclei with abundant pale pink/grey cytoplasm. Less intense purple than CT because the nucleus is smaller relative to the large foamy cytoplasm (macrophages are big cells).
🔄
Shape / arrangement
Nuclei are kidney-bean or horseshoe shaped (indented, not round). Cytoplasm looks "foamy" or "bubbly" — full of engulfed debris. Cells loosely scattered, not tightly packed like CT.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Less dense than CT. More pink overall. Cells look "happy" and round with space around them. Usually near necrotic zones (macrophages are cleaning up dead tissue). Foamy cytoplasm is the key giveaway.
6
PL
Presence of Lymphocytes (PL)
Tiny dark immune cells — rarest class (672 patches)
⚫
Colour
Very dark purple, almost black small dots. Lymphocytes are almost entirely nucleus — very little cytoplasm. The darkest cells on the slide.
🔵
Shape / arrangement
Tiny perfectly round dense nuclei — like small dark beads. Much smaller than tumor cells. Often in clusters around blood vessels ("perivascular cuffing") or scattered in groups.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Small clusters of tiny dark dots near vessels or in the tumor edge. Easy to miss — they're small. Look for the size contrast: lymphocytes are much smaller than tumor cells. Often a 128×128 ROI within the 512×512 patch.
3
LI
Leptomeningeal Infiltration (LI)
Tumor in the brain membranes — very rare (1,534 patches)
🩹
Colour
Mix of bright pink fibrous tissue (the meninges — collagen-rich, stains intensely with eosin) and dark purple tumor cell clusters invading it.
🌊
Shape / arrangement
A layered or sheet-like structure — the fibrous membrane layer is clearly visible as a pink band, with tumor cells growing along or through it. The tumor seems to "coat" the membrane surface.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Distinctive pink fibrous background (not seen in CT or NC) with dark purple invasion. The fibrous connective tissue layer is the landmark — tumor is infiltrating a structure that shouldn't have tumor cells.
2/8
IC&WM
Cortex Infiltration (IC) & White Matter (WM)
Tumor invading surrounding brain tissue — normal architecture visible
🩶
Colour
Mixed pink and purple — normal brain tissue (pale pink) with scattered infiltrating tumor cells (dark purple). Not uniformly dense like CT. The background is clearly "brain".
🏘️
Shape / arrangement
Normal brain architecture is still partially visible — you can see normal neurons/glial cells alongside infiltrating tumor cells. WM shows white matter fiber tract patterns. Tumor cells look "out of place" in an otherwise normal-looking background.
🗺️
Overall pattern
Sparse infiltrating cells — not the wall-to-wall density of CT. Normal tissue + scattered invaders. IC shows cortical layering. WM shows fiber tracks. The key: you can still see what's "supposed to be there".
The cross-site staining problem — why the same tissue looks different

This is the core reason the AI generalisation gap exists. The same class (e.g. cellular tumor) can look very different depending on which hospital scanned it and with which protocol. The visual features are the same biologically — but the colours shift significantly.

Hospital A
Deep violet
Strong haematoxylin — very purple, high contrast
Hospital B
Pinkish-red
Over-eosin — pink dominates, nuclei less distinct
Hospital C
Blue-shifted
Different scanner white balance — blue tint
Hospital D
Muted purple
Faded stain / older section — lower contrast

This is exactly why SAP-LeJEPA uses stain-invariant training: The context view gets stain augmentation (random colour jitter simulating different hospitals), but the target encoding stays in canonical stain space. The model learns "this pattern = cellular tumor" regardless of whether the purple is deep violet, pinkish, or blue-shifted. Without this, the model memorises the colour of Hospital A's stain — and fails on Hospital B's slides.

Quick visual reference — pathologist's mental checklist
  • 🟣
    Wall-to-wall dark purple, irregular nuclei → Cellular Tumor (CT)
  • ⬜
    Pale/white ghost zones, no nuclei → Geographic Necrosis (NC)
  • 🎯
    Purple cells in rings around pale centre → Pseudopalisading Necrosis (PN)
  • 🌀
    Tangled bright-pink vessel knots → Microvascular Proliferation (MP)
  • 🫘
    Kidney-bean nuclei, foamy cytoplasm → Dense Macrophages (DM)
  • ⚫
    Tiny very dark round dots near vessels → Lymphocytes (PL)
  • 🩹
    Pink fibrous sheet + invading purple cells → Leptomeningeal (LI)
  • 🏘️
    Normal brain + sparse infiltrating cells → IC or WM
  • 🩶
    Nothing specific, mixed appearance → NOTA (class 9)
  • 📏
    Overall density rule: CT > PN ring > IC/WM > DM > NC (palest)
