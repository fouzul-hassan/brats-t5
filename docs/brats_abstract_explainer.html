🧠 BraTS-Path Abstract — Visual Explainer

Every sentence, every domain term explained in plain English with visuals

Sentence 1 — The disease
"Glioblastoma is the most common primary adult brain tumor, with a grim prognosis — median survival of 12–18 months following treatment, and 4 months otherwise."
Term
Glioblastoma (GBM)
Cancer that starts in the brain itself, growing from glial cells (the brain's structural support cells). Most aggressive and most common malignant adult brain tumor. Even with full treatment — surgery + radiation + chemo — most patients survive under 18 months.
Term
Primary tumor
The cancer originated in the brain — not spread from somewhere else (lung, breast, etc.). "Secondary" or "metastatic" brain tumors came from outside. Primary tumors are fundamentally different in how they behave and are treated.
Sentence 2 — Why it's hard to treat
"Glioblastoma is widely infiltrative in the cerebral hemispheres and well-defined by heterogeneous molecular and micro-environmental histopathologic profiles, which pose a major obstacle in treatment."
Term
Widely infiltrative
The tumor does not have a clean round boundary. Its cells spread like tentacles into surrounding normal brain — making complete surgical removal impossible. You'd have to remove too much normal brain to get it all.
Term
Heterogeneous
Different regions of the same tumor look and behave completely differently under a microscope. One area is packed with live cancer cells, another is dead tissue, another is full of new blood vessels. This is the core challenge.
Term
Histopathologic profiles
Histo = tissue, patho = disease, logic = study. So: "the visual patterns seen when examining diseased tissue under a microscope." Each sub-region has a distinctive visual signature that expert pathologists recognize.
Term
Micro-environmental
The tumor's immediate surroundings at the cellular scale — immune cells attacking it, blood vessels feeding it, dead zones starving for oxygen. This "microenvironment" determines how the tumor grows and resists treatment.

Key insight: Because GBM is heterogeneous, a single biopsy from one spot doesn't tell the whole story. AI that can map the entire slide and identify all sub-regions gives clinicians a complete picture — not just one snapshot.

A tumor dominated by pseudopalisading necrosis (aggressive, hypoxic) needs different treatment than one mostly cellular tumor. Knowing the exact composition guides decisions on radiation dose, drug choice, and surgical approach.

Sentence 4 — The current diagnostic approach
"In the gold-standard histopathology-based approach to tumor diagnosis, detecting various morpho-pathological features of distinct histology throughout digitized tissue sections is crucial."
🔬
Gold standard = histopathology
The definitive diagnosis comes from examining actual tumor tissue under a microscope. Not MRI, not blood tests — the physical tissue slice, stained with dyes, examined by a specialist.
🧬
Morpho-pathological features
Morpho = shape/structure. These are the visual patterns that indicate disease: how densely packed cells are, dead zones, new blood vessel formations, immune cell clusters.
💻
Digitized tissue sections
A glass slide with tumor tissue is scanned at ~40,000×40,000 pixels to create a "Whole Slide Image" (WSI). This digital file is what the AI models analyze — and what the 80 unlabeled .tiff files are.
Sentence 5 — The 9 specific features (= 9 of your 10 classes)
"Such 'features' include the presence of cellular tumor, geographic necrosis, pseudopalisading necrosis, areas abundant in microvascular proliferation, infiltration into the cortex, wide extension in subcortical white matter, leptomeningeal infiltration, regions dense with macrophages, and the presence of lymphocytes."
The imbalance problem — why accuracy is a useless metric here

The paper explicitly flags class imbalance as a key challenge. CT has 65× more patches than PL. A model that predicts "CT" for everything would get 38% accuracy but be completely useless. This is why macro-F1 and MCC are the real metrics.

The central unsolved problem — the generalisation gap

The 2024 EfficientNet baseline revealed a catastrophic gap between training and deployment. This gap is what Hassan's SAP-LeJEPA targets.

Cross-validation F1 — same institution data (model has "seen" this style of image)

F1 = 0.98 ✓

Hidden test F1 — different institution, different scanner, different staining protocol

F1 = 0.52 ✗

Why? Hospital A's tissue looks pink-purple. Hospital B's looks more blue-violet. Same disease, same sub-region, but the AI thinks they're different. Stain-invariant training is the fix — which is exactly what the stain-aware view generation in SAP-LeJEPA does.

Sentence 6 & 7 — The challenge goal and clinical mission
"The goal of the BraTS-Path challenge is to provide a systematically prepared comprehensive dataset and a benchmarking environment to develop and fairly compare deep-learning models capable of identifying tumor sub-regions of distinct histologic profile. These models aim to further our understanding of the disease and assist in the diagnosis and grading of conditions in a consistent manner."
📦
Dataset
1M+ expert-labeled patches from 11 international institutions. Publicly available on Synapse. The largest annotated GBM histopathology dataset ever created.
⚖️
Benchmark
Same test set for everyone, same metrics. "My model beats yours" is now a verifiable scientific claim — not just a number on a different dataset.
🏥
Clinical impact
AI that maps an entire tumor slide in seconds vs. hours of pathologist time. Consistent — no inter-annotator variability. Can guide immunotherapy, radiation planning, and drug selection.

Where Hassan's work fits: SAP-LeJEPA is a submission to this benchmark. The scientific contribution is showing that principled in-domain self-supervised learning (LeJEPA + SIGReg + stain-aware masking) can close the 0.98 → 0.52 generalisation gap — which is the challenge's central unsolved problem.
